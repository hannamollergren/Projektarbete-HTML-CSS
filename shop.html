Moons

Mercury


The smallest planet in our solar system and nearest to the Sun, Mercury is only slightly larger than Earth's Moon.


Venus


Second planet from the Sun and our closest planetary neighbor, Venus is similar in structure and size to Earth, but it is now a very different world.


Earth


Our home planet is the third planet from the Sun, and the only place we know of so far that’s inhabited by living things.


Mars


The fourth planet from the Sun, Mars is a dusty, cold, desert world with a very thin atmosphere.


Jupiter


Jupiter has a long history surprising scientists—all the way back to 1610 when Galileo Galilei found the first moons beyond Earth. That discovery changed the way we see the universe.


Saturn


Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest planet in our solar system.


Uranus


The first planet found with the aid of a telescope, Uranus was discovered in 1781 by astronomer William Herschel, although he originally thought it was either a comet or a star.


Neptune


Dark, cold and whipped by supersonic winds, ice giant Neptune is the eighth and most distant planet in our solar system.


Deimos


Deimos is the smaller of Mars' two moons. Being only 9 by 7 by 6.8 miles in size (15 by 12 by 11 kilometers), Deimos whirls around Mars every 30 hours.


Io


Jupiter's moon Io is the most volcanically active world in the Solar System, with hundreds of volcanoes, some erupting lava fountains dozens of miles (or kilometers) high.


Ganymede


Ganymede is named for a boy who was made cupbearer for the ancient Greek gods by Zeus – Jupiter to the Romans.


Enceladus


Few worlds in our solar system are as compelling as Saturn’s icy ocean moon Enceladus.


Titan


Saturn’s largest moon Titan is an extraordinary and exceptional world.


Ariel


All of Uranus' larger moons, including Ariel, are thought to consist mostly of roughly equal amounts of water ice and silicate rock. Carbon dioxide has also been detected on Ariel.


Oberon


Oberon is the second largest moon of Uranus. Discovered in 1787, little was known about this moon until Voyager 2 passed it during its flyby of Uranus in January 1986.


Titania


Titania is Uranus' largest moon. Images taken by Voyager 2 almost 200 years after Titania's discovery revealed signs that the moon was geologically active.


Triton


Triton is the largest of Neptune's 13 moons. It is unusual because it is the only large moon in our solar system that orbits in the opposite direction of its planet's rotation―a retrograde orbit.
